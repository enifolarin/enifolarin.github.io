Follow-up Email copy on Copywriting class

Subject line: The court case of Carlill v Carbolic is as a result of a copywriting error.

Hi (first name),

Have you heard of the famous Carlill v Carbolic case. If you’re a stude, then you probably know this case like the back of your hands.

If you’re not, I will give you a summary of what it is about.

The defendant, the Carbolic Smoke Ball Company (imagine a company that provides cure for catarrh. Not exactly though, but that should give a clearer picture), placed an advertisement in a newspaper for their products, stating that any person who purchased and used their product but still contracted influenza (a flu) despite properly following the instructions would be entitled to a £100 reward.

The advert further stated that the company had demonstrated its sincerity by placing £1000 in a bank account to act as the reward.

This is where the problem ensued; the company argued that they could not be bound by the advert and that it was a mere ‘puff’

Now, let’s go back to the sole purpose of this letter.

The Advert was a really clever one. It fulfilled most, if not all of the basics of a good ad structure.

The ad makes the price visible enough that whoever reads it notices it quickly and is caught by it, ‘£100 REWARD’, very clever

Anyone who sees that will immediately say, ‘okay what’s this about?’ And that’s how you start to get a customer’s attention.

The ad makes the Bold promise believable by adding that the reward is already deposited in the bank, so they didn’t have to worry about not being rewarded.

This automatically makes the customer think, ‘these people must be so sure of their product, it must be a good one.

And if it doesn’t work, I can claim my reward. There’s nothing to lose’.

And that’s just what you need to make anybody in the world feel in order for them to take the action you need them to. ‘Nothing to lose’.

Also, notice that the ad highlights the benefits of the product, which is just perfect.

This ad will immediately make a shitload of sales and it did make a shitload of sale.

Where then is the error?

An important thing in using Bold promises is being able to fulfill them.

The ad failed the moment a customer used the Smoke ball and still caught influenza, and it failed even more when they were not able to give out the reward.

Moral lesson? You must know where this is coming to by now. Bold promise is a very effective copywriting angle, in fact, one of the best as long as you can make it believable.

But make sure your promise is one that you or your product can fulfill.

You don’t want to spend years on a court case just because you wanted to sound cool.
